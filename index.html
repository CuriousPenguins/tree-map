About the Project

This project republishes the Annex tree survey as an interactive map built with OpenStreetMap, Leaflet, and open GeoJSON data. The goal is to make it easy for residents, researchers, and city enthusiasts to explore the urban forest of the Annex.

The data is stored in a single GeoJSON file: 

https://raw.githubusercontent.com/ptoone/annex-tree-map/refs/heads/main/data/annex-trees.json

Everyone is welcome to help correct errors, add missing details, or build new tools that reuse this dataset.

Data Source & Credits

The Annex tree data was originally collected as part of a neighbourhood tree survey in Toronto‚Äôs Annex, carried out by local volunteers and community members between approximately 2010 and 2014.

Volunteers from The Ara recorded species, address, DBH (Diameter at Breast Height), height, and other details for each tree, and the results were published as an interactive map on a community website.

This GitHub project:

  • Republishes the survey data in an open GeoJSON format.
  • Provides an updated interactive map using OpenStreetMap and Leaflet.
  • Invites the community to help clean, document, and extend the dataset.

If you use this dataset or embed the map, please acknowledge the original survey organizers and volunteers, and consider contributing improvements back via GitHub.

Easy Ways to Measure a Tree‚Äôs Height

You don‚Äôt need special equipment to estimate a tree‚Äôs height for the Annex Tree Map. Here are four simple methods that use everyday tools like your phone, a stick, or shadows.

This method uses your smartphone‚Äôs built-in compass or a free clinometer app. It‚Äôs one of the most accurate DIY options.

You need: a smartphone and a way to measure or pace out distance.

  1. Stand a known distance from the tree (e.g., 10 m or 33 ft).
  2. Open your phone‚Äôs level/angle/clinometer tool.
  3. Hold the phone at eye height and aim at the highest point of the tree.
  4. Note the angle (Œ∏) displayed on the screen.
  5. Use this formula: 
    Tree height ‚âà (Distance √ó tan Œ∏) + Eye height

Example:

  • Distance to tree: 10 m
  • Angle: 40¬∞
  • Eye height: 1.6 m

Tree height ‚âà (10 √ó tan 40¬∞) + 1.6 ‚âà 10 m

This works best on a sunny day and uses simple proportions.

  1. Measure the length of the tree‚Äôs shadow.
  2. Measure the shadow of a known object, like yourself or a stick of known height.
  3. Use the proportional formula: 
    Tree height = (Tree shadow √∑ Your shadow) √ó Your height

Example:

  • Tree shadow: 12 m
  • Your shadow: 2 m
  • Your height: 1.75 m

Tree height = (12 √∑ 2) √ó 1.75 = 10.5 m

This uses a stick or pencil at arm‚Äôs length to create a simple similar-triangles setup.

You need: a straight stick, pencil, or ruler.

  1. Hold the stick vertically at arm‚Äôs length, so it looks like it ‚Äúcovers‚Äù the tree.
  2. Walk closer or farther until the top of the stick lines up with the top of the tree and the bottom lines up with the base.
  3. When the stick visually matches the height of the tree, stop and mark where you stand.
  4. Measure or pace the distance from your standing point to the base of the tree.

That distance is approximately the height of the tree.

This classic field trick works surprisingly well and needs no tools at all.

  1. Face the tree and walk away from it.
  2. Bend forward so you‚Äôre looking between your legs back toward the tree.
  3. Slowly walk away from the tree until you can just see the top of the tree between your legs.
  4. Stop and mark that spot.
  5. Measure or pace the distance from that spot back to the tree.

That distance is roughly the height of the tree.

Measuring DBH (Diameter at Breast Height)

DBH is a standard way to describe tree size. It means the diameter of the trunk measured at about chest height. Here‚Äôs how anyone can measure it for the Annex Tree Map.

DBH (Diameter at Breast Height) is the diameter of the tree trunk measured at 1.3 m (about 4.5 ft) above the ground on the uphill side of the tree.

We use DBH because it‚Äôs a simple, repeatable way for different people to describe the size of the same tree. If you can, measure to the nearest centimetre (or half-inch).

This is the simplest method if you have a flexible measuring tape.

  1. Stand on the uphill side of the tree (if the ground is sloped).
  2. Find 1.3 m / 4.5 ft above the ground. This is breast height.
  3. Wrap the tape around the trunk at that height to measure the circumference.
  4. Record the circumference (C) in centimetres or inches.
  5. Convert circumference to diameter using: 
    Diameter = Circumference √∑ œÄ ‚âà Circumference √∑ 3.14

Example:

  • Circumference: 94 cm

Diameter ‚âà 94 √∑ 3.14 ‚âà 30 cm DBH

You can still measure DBH even without a tape measure.

  1. At 1.3 m / 4.5 ft above the ground, wrap a string, shoelace, belt, or cord around the trunk once.
  2. Mark or pinch the point where it completes the circle.
  3. Lay the string flat and measure its length with any ruler or tape at home.
  4. That length is the circumference. Convert to diameter with: 
    Diameter = Circumference √∑ 3.14

If you don‚Äôt want to do the math, you can simply submit the circumference and we can convert it to DBH later.

Not all trees are straight and simple. Here are some quick guidelines:

  • Tree on a slope: Measure 1.3 m / 4.5 ft from the ground on the uphill side of the tree.
  • Big bump or branch right at breast height: Move the measuring point a little up or down until you find the nearest ‚Äúnormal‚Äù section of trunk and note this.
  • Leaning tree: Still measure along the trunk at 1.3 m / 4.5 ft from the ground, not straight vertically.
  • Multi-stem tree (splits near the base): If several stems split below breast height, measure the diameter of each main stem separately and note that it is a multi-stem tree.

If you‚Äôre unsure, you can add a short note in your submission (e.g. ‚Äúmulti-stem maple, DBH for largest stem ‚âà 25 cm‚Äù).
